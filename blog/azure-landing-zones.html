Azure Landing Zones: the platform foundation

An Azure landing zone is an environment prepared in advance to host your workloads — with identity, networking, governance, security and management already in place — so applications land somewhere consistent, secure and ready to scale. This guide covers the conceptual architecture, the management-group hierarchy, the eight design areas, network topology, design principles and how to actually deploy one.

Henderson Andrade Published 17 Jun 2026 ~18 min read Azure · Landing Zones · Platform
Landing Zones Management groups 8 design areas Hub-spoke Bicep · Terraform

What a landing zone actually is

Picture two organizations adopting Azure. The first lets every team create subscriptions on demand, wire their own networks and set their own policies. Six months in, it's a sprawl of inconsistent naming, ungoverned spend, flat networks and no idea where the data lives. The second deploys a landing zone first: a pre-built environment where identity, network connectivity, policy guardrails, security tooling and a logging baseline already exist. Every new workload "lands" into that environment and inherits all of it.

A landing zone is the concrete output of the Cloud Adoption Framework's Ready methodology. It is multi-subscription by design, scales with your organization, and is governed centrally while still giving application teams the autonomy they need.

ℹ
Landing zones are the "Ready" deliverable of the broader journey. For the full picture — Strategy, Plan, Adopt, Govern, Manage, Secure — read Cloud Adoption Framework for Azure: a practitioner's deep dive.

Platform vs. application landing zones

The single most important distinction. A platform landing zone provides shared services that everything else depends on — identity, management and connectivity — owned by the central platform team. Application landing zones are the subscriptions where workloads actually run, handed to application teams with guardrails already applied.

The conceptual architecture

Microsoft publishes a conceptual architecture for Azure landing zones — an opinionated, proven target you can deploy as-is and adapt. It's expressed primarily as a management group hierarchy with policies assigned at each level, plus the platform and application subscriptions beneath it.

The management group hierarchy

Management groups are containers above subscriptions where you assign Azure Policy and RBAC once and have them inherited downward. The canonical Azure landing zone hierarchy looks like this:

Management groupHoldsWhy separate
PlatformIdentity, Management, Connectivity subsShared services with their own stricter policies.
Landing zones → CorpInternal apps (no public ingress)Apps that connect to on-prem / are internal-only.
Landing zones → OnlineInternet-facing appsPublic workloads needing edge/WAF policy.
SandboxExperimentation subsFreedom to try, isolated from production policy.
DecommissionedSubs being retiredLocked-down holding area before deletion.

The eight design areas

Every landing zone implementation is shaped by eight design areas. They are the decisions you must make (or inherit from the accelerator) before workloads arrive. Think of them as the checklist for a production-ready platform.

#Design areaKey decisions
AAzure billing & Entra tenantEA/MCA structure, tenant count, enterprise enrollment.
BIdentity & access managementEntra ID, RBAC model, PIM, hybrid identity.
CResource organizationManagement group tree, subscription strategy, naming & tagging.
DNetwork topology & connectivityHub-spoke vs Virtual WAN, firewall, private DNS, hybrid links.
ESecurityDefender for Cloud, encryption, Key Vault, secrets baseline.
FManagementMonitoring, Log Analytics, backup, DR, Azure Arc for hybrid.
GGovernanceAzure Policy guardrails, cost management, compliance.
HPlatform automation & DevOpsIaC, pipelines, the platform's own GitOps lifecycle.

Network topology — hub & spoke

The most common landing zone network pattern is hub-and-spoke (with Virtual WAN as the Microsoft-managed alternative at scale). A central hub in the Connectivity subscription holds shared network services — firewall, gateways, DNS — and each workload sits in its own spoke VNet, peered to the hub. Traffic between spokes and to on-prem/internet is inspected centrally.

Design principles

The conceptual architecture is built on a handful of principles worth internalizing:

  • Subscription democratization. Use subscriptions as a unit of management and scale, handed to teams — not as a scarce resource.
  • Policy-driven governance. Use Azure Policy to enforce and auto-remediate guardrails, instead of manual gatekeeping.
  • Single control & management plane. Govern consistently through Azure Resource Manager; avoid bypass tooling.
  • Application-centric. Optimize for application migration and innovation, not just raw infrastructure.
  • Align with Azure-native design & roadmap. Prefer platform-native services so you ride Microsoft's roadmap.
  • Plan for growth & recovery. Build a topology that scales to many subscriptions without re-architecture.

Implementation options

You don't build a landing zone from a blank page — Microsoft ships accelerators. Pick the path that matches how your team works:

OptionBest forNotes
Portal acceleratorGetting started fastGuided ARM-based deployment of the full conceptual architecture.
ALZ BicepAzure-native IaC teamsModular Bicep; Microsoft-maintained (Azure/ALZ-Bicep).
ALZ TerraformMulti-cloud / Terraform shopsOfficial modules via the Azure Verified Modules / aka.ms/alz/tf.
Start small & expandBrownfield / cautious rolloutBegin with a minimal MG + policy set, grow toward the full design.
⚠
Whatever you choose, manage it as code from day one. A landing zone clicked together by hand drifts the moment two people touch it — the whole point is a repeatable, version-controlled platform.
ℹ
Once deployed, keep it honest: use AzureUtils and the Azure Estate Report to spot drift, orphaned resources and tag gaps against your landing zone baseline.

A quick self-review checklist

  • Structure — Management group hierarchy in place (Platform / Landing zones / Sandbox / Decommissioned)?
  • Platform — Identity, Management and Connectivity subscriptions separated and owned by the platform team?
  • Subscriptions — Application landing zones handed out with policy already applied (democratized, not ad-hoc)?
  • Network — Hub-and-spoke or Virtual WAN with centralized firewall, DNS and hybrid connectivity?
  • Governance — Azure Policy guardrails assigned at the MG level with auto-remediation?
  • Automation — Entire platform deployed and maintained as code (accelerator / Bicep / Terraform)?
  • Design areas — All eight areas consciously decided, not left to chance?
