Cloud Adoption Framework for Azure: a practitioner's deep dive

The Microsoft Cloud Adoption Framework (CAF) is the end-to-end guidance for taking an organization to Azure — from business strategy all the way to ongoing operations and governance. Where the Well-Architected Framework makes a single workload excellent, CAF makes the whole cloud journey deliberate. This guide walks every methodology with diagrams, practical patterns, the supporting tools and the official Microsoft references.

Henderson Andrade Published 17 Jun 2026 ~17 min read Azure · CAF · Cloud adoption
Cloud Adoption Strategy Plan Ready Adopt Govern · Manage · Secure

What the Cloud Adoption Framework is

Most failed cloud programs don't fail on technology — they fail on alignment: a migration with no business case, a platform built before anyone agreed on governance, costs that nobody owns. The Cloud Adoption Framework is Microsoft's answer to that. It is a collection of documentation, best practices, tools and reference implementations that take you through the cloud journey in a structured, repeatable way, aligning business, people and technology at each step.

CAF is organized into methodologies. Four are sequential phases of the journey — Strategy, Plan, Ready and Adopt — and three are continuous disciplines that run for as long as you're in the cloud — Govern, Manage and Secure. You don't do CAF once; you cycle through it as your estate grows.

CAF vs. Well-Architected — two altitudes

These two frameworks are constantly confused. The simplest mental model: CAF is about the organization and the platform; WAF is about the workload. CAF gets your tenant, subscriptions, governance and operating model ready and moves applications in. WAF then makes each of those applications reliable, secure, cost-effective, operable and performant.

Phase 1 Strategy

Everything starts with why. The Strategy methodology forces you to document your motivations (cost savings, exiting a datacenter, agility, new markets, scale, risk reduction) and translate them into measurable business outcomes. Without this, every later decision is a guess — and you'll struggle to know if the program is succeeding.

What you produce

  • Documented motivations. Why now, and what business pressure is driving the move.
  • Business outcomes & KPIs. Concrete, measurable goals tied to revenue, cost, agility or risk.
  • A business case. The financial justification — TCO, expected ROI and timeline.
  • First project. A well-chosen initial workload that proves value and builds momentum.
Key takeaway

No outcome, no project. If you can't state the measurable business result a move to Azure will deliver, you're not ready to spend on it yet.

Phase 2 Plan

Plan converts strategy into an actionable, rational plan. The centrepiece is the digital estate — an inventory and rationalization of everything you might move — combined with organizational alignment and a skilling plan, all rolled into a prioritized backlog you can actually execute.

The "5 Rs" of rationalization

StrategyWhat it meansWhen
Rehost"Lift & shift" the workload as-is to IaaS.Speed over optimization; datacenter exit.
RefactorMinor changes to use PaaS (e.g. App Service, managed DB).Quick efficiency wins.
RearchitectSignificantly evolve the app (e.g. to microservices).App can't scale / is hard to maintain.
RebuildRewrite cloud-native from scratch.Legacy beyond saving; big upside.
ReplaceRetire and adopt SaaS instead.A commodity capability (email, CRM).

What you produce

  • Digital estate. Inventory + rationalization (the 5 Rs) of workloads and assets.
  • Organizational alignment. Who owns the cloud — a cloud team / center of excellence.
  • Skills readiness plan. Close the gap between today's skills and what the plan needs.
  • Cloud adoption plan. A prioritized, time-boxed backlog (often in Azure Boards).

Phase 3 Ready

Ready prepares the environment before workloads arrive. Its core deliverable is the Azure landing zone: an environment that already has identity, networking, governance, security and management baked in, so every workload lands somewhere safe, consistent and ready to scale — instead of teams hand-rolling subscriptions in a hundred different ways.

What "Ready" gives you

  • Azure landing zones. Platform foundation (identity, management, connectivity) + application landing zones.
  • A scalable structure. Management groups and subscriptions designed to grow without rework.
  • Policy-driven guardrails. Azure Policy enforcing the baseline from day one.
  • Accelerators. Reference implementations (portal accelerator, Bicep, Terraform) instead of a blank page.

Phase 4 Adopt

Adopt is where workloads actually move. It splits into two motions that usually run in parallel: Migrate (bring existing workloads over) and Innovate (build new, cloud-native capabilities). Most organizations do both — migrate the estate to get out of the datacenter, and innovate where the cloud unlocks new business value.

Continuous Govern

Governance is the set of guardrails that keep cloud adoption from turning into cloud chaos. CAF frames it as five disciplines — areas of corporate policy you translate into automated controls (largely via Azure Policy). Governance is risk-driven: you invest in the disciplines proportional to the risk each one is mitigating, and you tighten them as the estate grows.

DisciplineMitigatesAzure controls
Cost ManagementBudget overruns, wasteBudgets, Cost Management, tag policy.
Security BaselineData & config exposureAzure Policy, Defender for Cloud, Secure Score.
Identity BaselineUnauthorized accessEntra ID, RBAC, Conditional Access, PIM.
Resource ConsistencyConfig drift, orphansPolicy, management groups, naming/tagging.
Deployment AccelerationManual, inconsistent deploysIaC (Bicep/Terraform), blueprints, CI/CD.

Continuous Manage

Manage establishes an operations baseline — the minimum, consistent operations management applied across the whole estate — and then layers richer operations on top for the workloads that justify it. It's where CAF hands off to day-2 reliability and where the Well-Architected Operational Excellence pillar lives in practice.

What you establish

  • Operations baseline. Inventory, monitoring, patching, backup and DR applied estate-wide.
  • Business commitments. Map workloads to criticality, then to SLA / RTO / RPO targets.
  • Operations maturity. Advance from reactive break-fix toward platform & workload operations.
  • Tooling. Azure Monitor, Update Manager, Azure Backup, Site Recovery and Azure Arc for hybrid.

Continuous Secure

Secure is CAF's end-to-end security methodology, aligned to Zero Trust. It isn't a one-off phase — it threads security through strategy, planning, the landing zone and operations, covering risk insights, security controls and continuous resilience. It complements the Well-Architected Security pillar at the organizational scale.

What it covers

  • Risk insights. Understand business risk and feed it into security priorities.
  • Security controls. Identity, network, data and application protections as baseline policy.
  • Security operations (SecOps). Detect, respond and recover — Defender for Cloud + Sentinel.
  • Continuous improvement. Track Secure Score and raise the baseline over time.

Getting started — a pragmatic roadmap

You don't need to "finish" Strategy before touching Azure. A practical sequence:

The tools to know

ToolWhere it helps
Cloud Adoption Framework hubGuidance, templates and the Strategy/Plan templates.
Azure landing zone acceleratorDeploy a conformant platform foundation fast (portal / IaC).
Azure MigrateDiscovery, assessment and migration of servers, DBs and apps.
Governance benchmark & Azure PolicyAssess governance maturity; enforce the five disciplines.

A quick self-review checklist

  • Strategy — Motivations documented? At least one measurable business outcome with a KPI?
  • Plan — Digital estate inventoried and rationalized (5 Rs)? A cloud team / CoE named? Skilling plan?
  • Ready — Azure landing zone deployed via an accelerator? Management groups & policy baseline in place?
  • Adopt — Migrate and innovate motions defined? Azure Migrate used for assessment?
  • Govern — The five disciplines mapped to Azure Policy? Budgets and tagging enforced?
  • Manage — Operations baseline (monitor, patch, backup, DR) applied estate-wide?
  • Secure — Defender for Cloud on, Secure Score tracked, SecOps responsibilities clear?
